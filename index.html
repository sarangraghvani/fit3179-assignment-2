Australia's Economy Compared To The World

What Is The Size Of Different Economies?


Around the world, some countries have large economies and others are smaller. So how can we measure and compare them to Australia?

Well the Gross domestic product (GDP) is a universally accepted metric. It is simply a monetary measure of the market value of of all fiscal goods and services traded in each financial year. So it is calculated as the sum of consumption by households, investment by businesses, government spending on goods and services and net exports.

But if we simply use this GDP to compare countries around the world, then countries with high populations such as China and the US would be shown disproportionately as compared to smaller countries like Australia. So a non-trivial solution is to normalise the GDP with the population. This is called the GDP per Capita.

From the map we can see that Australia's GDP per Capita is relatively high as is the case with many other developed economies such as the US, Canada and New Zealand. This means that generally Australian's currently have good purchasing power and are relatively wealthy today. Has this always been the case throughout history?
Looking through GDP per capita over time from 1960 back when Sir Robert Menzies the Prime Minister, to 2021 today, we can see that generally over time, comparable countries have increased the size of their respective economies as has Australia .

Back then Australia's GDP per capita was $1,811 and now it is upto almost a whopping $60,000!

However GDP growth is not steady but is rather cyclical in nature. Economies boom then recessions follow and this cycle repeats. From the year 1995 to 2000 there was the Dot Com Bubble and in 2008 there was the Global Financial Crisis which saw all major economies contract.

While other countries have gone through many recessions, Australia didn't have a recession for almost 30 years until now. This proves that Australia's economy is resilient even in bad times and Infact, there was a time when it had the highest GDP per capita among these nations.

So why is Australia's economy so resiliant? It must be something to do with the community.

How Are The Societal Factors?


A country and its society is generally more prosperous when corruption is low and unemployment is low. This underpins an efficient and strong economy.

We can see that countries that have low corruption levels and an low unemployments have greater GDP per capitas.

Australia has a corruption index of 27 and an unemployment of 5% and thus has a high GDP per capita. This is also true for places like the US and Western Europe.

Overall, the pattern is obvious and therefore we can see that to improve a countries economic postion, their respective governments must tackle corruption and maximise the working population.

Internal Economy

Looking within Australia's economy, so how can we see which state or territory is overperforming and which is dragging the economy down?

We can observe the proportion of gross state products contributing to the overall countries GDP, and compare that to the relative population.
Comparing the states and territories we can see that Western Australia (WA) only has 10% of the countries population yet contributes to upwards of 20% of GDP.

WA is outperforming other states due to its strong mining industry, which has strong growth of 4.1% year on year. Looking at other industries, the IT, science and healthcare sector have the most
growth and thus other states such as Vic and NSW can look to those industries in an attempt to increase their economic output.

Overall, Australia's economy is very strong and their population do reap the benefits, infact throughout the countries economy has been experiencing high growth and we can expect that to continue into the forseeable future!
